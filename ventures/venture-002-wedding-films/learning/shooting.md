# Cinematic Shooting — Venture 002 (Wedding Films)

Camera craft, built one shoot at a time on an iPhone 15. No cinema camera is coming to save this. Every rung below is a real session with a real export — you do not advance by reading, you advance by standing up and filming something.

This track exists for one reason: to feed the Cinematic Editing track raw material it can't get anywhere else. Sloppy coverage caps every edit you'll ever cut from it. Master this track and your editing ceiling rises with it.

The runtime reads each rung's **Goal / Lesson / Do / Milestone / Review**. Everything else below each rung (Why this matters, What to observe, Success criteria, Common mistakes, Stretch challenge) is mentor notes for you — read them before you shoot, not after.

---

## Rung 1 — Stable & sharp (the non-negotiables)
*Sprint Day 1, evening · ~30 min · pure reps, no deliverable pressure*

**Goal:** Every clip you shoot is steady, in focus, and correctly exposed — with zero exceptions.
**Lesson:** Cinematic starts with technically clean, not with taste. A beautifully composed shot that's soft or shaky is unusable; a boring shot that's steady and sharp is editable. Lock exposure and focus so the phone stops hunting mid-take. Brace your body — not the shot — so the frame holds. Shoot 4K at 50/60fps so you have the option of smooth slow-motion later, even if you don't use it today.
**Why this matters:** Professionals are ruthless about this because the cost of a soft clip is total — there is no fixing focus in post. The Quirky, Iris, Daniel Schaffer: every one of their "simple" shots is rock-solid first. Style is built on top of competence, never instead of it.
**What to observe:** Watch any 10 seconds of professional wedding work and notice what you *don't* see — no micro-jitter, no exposure hunting, no soft-to-sharp racking unless it's a deliberate rack focus. Stillness is invisible when it's done right; that invisibility is the skill.
**Do:**
- In the camera app, set video to 4K 50/60 fps.
- Tap-and-hold to lock AE/AF on a subject; practise re-locking as it moves.
- Film 5 ten-second clips holding the phone braced against your body or a wall.
- Film the same 5 clips again using a rail, ledge, or cheap tripod for stability.
**Milestone:** 5 rock-steady, sharp, well-exposed 10s clips.
**Success criteria:** Scrub every clip frame-by-frame at 2x. Zero visible shake, zero hunting focus, no blown highlights or crushed shadows on skin or key subject.
**Common mistakes:** Locking exposure once and never re-checking as the subject moves into shade or light; holding the phone away from the body (always shakier than braced-in); shooting 24fps "for the cinematic look" before you've proven you can hold a shot steady — earn the frame rate choice later, don't hide behind it now.
**Stretch challenge (if you finish early):** Shoot one 15s clip walking slowly while staying locked on a subject — the hardest steady-shot category. If it's usable, you've already exceeded this rung.
**Review:** Scrub each clip. Any shake, soft focus, or blown highlights? Note which bracing method worked best — that becomes your default.

## Rung 2 — Composition & framing
*Sprint Day 2, session 1 of 2 · ~40 min*

**Goal:** Frame shots that look intentional, not snapshots.
**Lesson:** Use the rule of thirds, leading lines, and foreground depth. Leave space where movement or eyes are going — a subject looking into empty frame feels wrong; a subject looking into space feels composed. Shoot wide / medium / close of the same subject every time, because the editor (you, in three days) needs coverage to cut between, not just one pretty angle.
**Why this matters:** Study any high-end wedding teaser and you'll notice it almost never holds on one size for more than a couple of seconds — wide establishes, medium carries action, close carries emotion. If you only shoot one size, the edit has nowhere to go.
**What to observe:** Today's reference study — The Quirky and Ramit Batra (see `references.md`). Don't just watch; freeze-frame three random shots from each and ask: where's the subject in the frame, what's in the foreground, what's the background doing. Write down the pattern you see repeated.
**What to shoot:** A friend, a café table, a street corner — any subject with depth available (doorway, plant, railing) to frame through.
**Do:**
- Turn on the camera grid.
- Shoot one subject three ways: wide (context), medium (action), close (detail).
- Frame three shots using a doorway, arch, or branches as a foreground frame.
- Place a moving subject on a third-line with space ahead of them.
**Milestone:** A set of 9 clips (3 subjects × wide/medium/close) that cut together cleanly.
**Success criteria:** Lay the 9 clips on a DaVinci timeline in size order (wide→medium→close) for each subject. If it already feels like a mini-sequence without any cutting tricks, the coverage is correct.
**Common mistakes:** Shooting three sizes of three *different* moments instead of the same one — that's not coverage, it's just more footage; centering every subject out of habit instead of choice; framing through an object so heavily it just looks like an obstruction, not a frame.
**Stretch challenge:** Shoot a fourth size — an extreme close-up (hands, eyes, a detail) — for one of your three subjects. Notice how much more an edit can do with four sizes than three.
**Review:** Do the three sizes of each subject edit together without a jump? Note your strongest composition.

## Rung 3 — Movement (motivated camera moves)
*Sprint Day 2, session 2 of 2 · ~30 min*

**Goal:** Add motion that feels smooth and has a reason to exist.
**Lesson:** A move should reveal something — a slow push builds intimacy, a reveal from behind an object creates a small surprise, a parallax slide adds depth that a static shot can't. Move slowly and finish the move completely; an unfinished move (one that just stops) reads as a mistake, not a style. Unmotivated movement — moving the camera because you're bored holding it still — is the single fastest way to look amateur on screen.
**Why this matters:** The Quirky's signature is restraint in movement: most of their frame is locked, and the few moves that exist are slow and purposeful, which is exactly why they land. Movement is a sentence, not noise — it should say one thing.
**What to observe:** In your reference clips from Rung 2, time exactly how long each push or reveal takes from start to finish, and how long the camera holds still before and after. Pros rest on either side of a move far more than beginners expect.
**Do:**
- Practise a slow, even push-in toward a detail (walk heel-to-toe).
- Shoot a reveal: start hidden behind a pillar/plant, then slide to expose the subject.
- Shoot a parallax slide past a close foreground object.
- Re-shoot your favourite at half speed for slow-motion smoothness.
**Milestone:** Three smooth, motivated moving shots (push, reveal, parallax).
**Success criteria:** Play each move at full speed. If you can name, in one sentence, what the move revealed or built emotionally, it passes. If the answer is "nothing, it just moves," reshoot it static-first, then add the move back only once you know why.
**Common mistakes:** Starting the move before the camera has settled (the first half-second is shaky and unusable); rushing the push so it overshoots and has to correct; moving and zooming digitally at the same time, which doubles the shake.
**Stretch challenge:** Shoot one move that combines a push with a reveal — moving toward a subject who is only exposed midway through the move. This is the hardest combination move in this rung; most amateur footage never attempts it.
**Review:** Are the moves slow and finished? Note any jerk at the start/stop to smooth next time.

## Rung 4 — Light (shape the mood)
*Sprint Day 3, session 1 of 3 · ~30 min · study: House on the Clouds*

**Goal:** Read and use light deliberately so footage looks rich instead of flat.
**Lesson:** Soft, directional light flatters everything. Window light and golden hour are free and beautiful; harsh midday sun and overhead indoor light are the enemy of every cinematic look you're chasing this week. Put the light to the side of the subject, never behind your phone — front-lighting is the flattest, least cinematic choice available to you and it's also the one beginners default to.
**Why this matters:** House on the Clouds is built almost entirely on light discipline — soft window light, golden hour, and an evident refusal to shoot in anything flat or harsh. That restraint, not gear, is what makes their frames look expensive. You have the same window light they do.
**What to observe:** Watch one House on the Clouds piece and count how many shots are lit from directly in front of the subject. The number will be close to zero. Notice instead how often the light source is *in* the frame or just out of it — windows, doorways, open sky.
**Do:**
- Shoot the same subject in harsh midday sun, then in open shade — compare.
- Shoot a portrait lit by a window from the side.
- Film 3 clips in the golden hour (first/last hour of sun).
- Indoors, turn off mixed overheads and shoot near one soft source.
**Milestone:** A 4-clip "same subject, four lights" comparison + one genuinely beautiful golden-hour shot.
**Success criteria:** Show the four-light comparison to one person without telling them which is "supposed" to be best. If they pick the side-lit or golden-hour clip unprompted, the lesson landed.
**Common mistakes:** Treating "golden hour" as good enough by default and not still checking direction — golden light from behind your phone is still flat; mixing window light and overhead bulbs in one frame, which fights the white balance and looks muddy; shooting into bright windows without exposing for the subject's face.
**Stretch challenge:** Find one harsh-light situation today (midday sun) and solve it without waiting for better light — use a doorway, an awning, or your own shadow as a diffuser, and shoot a usable clip anyway. Real shoots don't wait for golden hour.
**Review:** Which light looked most cinematic and why? Write the lighting rule you'll repeat — this becomes your default setup.

## Rung 5 — Coverage for a story (shoot like an editor)
*Sprint Day 3, session 2 of 3 · ~45 min · study: White in Revery · feeds Deliverable #2*

**Goal:** Walk into any scene and leave with everything an edit needs — nothing more is shot today than what gets cut into the Cinematic Sequence this evening.
**Lesson:** Edits die without coverage, not without talent. Bring a shot list: establishers, details, people, and the hero moment. Get b-roll of hands, textures, and reactions — always shoot more detail than feels necessary, because the editor (you, tonight) will want it and there's no second chance once the moment passes.
**Why this matters:** White in Revery's edits feel observational and unforced because the shoot behind them wasn't — it was thoroughly covered, so the edit could afford to feel loose. Effortless-looking footage is the most planned footage on this list.
**What to observe:** In a White in Revery piece, count how many genuinely candid (unposed, unaware-of-camera) moments appear versus posed ones. The ratio is higher than people assume — that's coverage discipline, catching real moments rather than staging all of them.
**What to shoot:** Stage a small real scene — a friend making coffee, a short walk, a meal at a café, a casual hangout — anything with people, hands, and a clear beginning/middle/end you can plan a 10-shot list around.
**Do:**
- Write a 10-shot list before a small staged or real event.
- Capture at least one wide establisher and five detail close-ups.
- Get three "people" shots: a candid, a reaction, an interaction.
- Shoot the one hero moment two different ways.
**Milestone:** Full coverage of one mini-scene that you can cut into the 30–45s Cinematic Sequence (Deliverable #2) tonight.
**Success criteria:** Before you stop shooting, scroll your shot list and check every line has a clip against it. A missing line found in the edit costs you the whole evening; a missing line found now costs sixty seconds.
**Common mistakes:** Shooting the hero moment only once and moving on — you rarely get the best version on the first take; skipping detail shots because "I'll remember to grab one later" (you won't, the moment passes); over-directing a candid shot until it's visibly posed, which kills the exact quality you were trying to capture.
**Stretch challenge:** Add one more size of your hero moment beyond the two required — a third angle or distance — and use whichever cuts best tonight, discarding the other two without sentiment.
**Review:** In the edit, was anything missing? Add it to your standard shot list — this list compounds across every future shoot this week.

## Rule
A rung is complete only when the milestone footage exists on your timeline. You learn shooting by shooting, not by reading settings. If a rung's milestone isn't shot today, the fix tomorrow is to cut scope elsewhere — not to skip the milestone.
