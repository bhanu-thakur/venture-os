# Editing Curriculum — Venture 002 (Wedding Films)

A skill ladder, not a course. Each rung is one loop: **learn a little → do the real thing → ship a portfolio-quality piece → review it**. You advance a rung only when something is finished and shown to one real person — not when you "understand" it.

The runtime reads the rungs below and walks you through them one step at a time. Keep each `**Lesson** / **Challenge** / **Deliverable** / **Review**` line present so the Learning Engine can guide you.

## Rung 1 — The cut (rhythm & story)
**Lesson:** Every wedding edit is built from four beats — establish (where we are), detail (the small human things), emotion (the look, the tear, the laugh), payoff (the moment everyone waited for). Cut length controls feeling: long holds breathe, fast cuts lift energy. Don't cut on action; cut on emotion.
**Challenge:** Pick one reference film from `references.md`. Watch its first 30 seconds three times, counting every cut and noting where the music turns. Rebuild that *timing* in DaVinci with your own or stock footage — match the rhythm, not the footage.
**Deliverable:** One 20–30s reconstructed sequence whose pacing matches the reference. Exported, watchable, rough is fine.
**Review:** Play yours and the reference back to back. Where does yours drag or rush? Write the one timing fix you'd make next time.

## Rung 2 — Music & sync
**Lesson:** Music is the spine, not the wallpaper. Cut major moments onto beats; let audio lead the eye with J-cuts (sound comes in early) and L-cuts (sound lingers out). Silence and a single held breath before a swell is a tool.
**Challenge:** Choose one track. Mark its beats and its one big swell. Build a 45–60s piece where every major cut lands on a musical moment and the emotional peak hits the swell.
**Deliverable:** A 45–60s beat-synced cut. Every hard cut should feel "on time" when watched without thinking about it.
**Review:** Watch with eyes closed once — does the *sound* alone tell the story arc? Note one cut that fights the music.

## Rung 3 — Colour (the cinematic look)
**Lesson:** Grade in order: balance (neutral, correct exposure/white) → contrast (depth) → look (a deliberate mood). One consistent look beats ten clever ones. Build looks you can reuse so grading takes minutes, not hours.
**Challenge:** Take your Rung 2 piece into DaVinci's colour page. Balance every shot, then apply one deliberate look (warm filmic, soft teal-orange, etc.). Save it as a reusable power-grade / LUT.
**Deliverable:** The Rung 2 piece, fully graded, plus one saved reusable look.
**Review:** Toggle the grade on/off. Does it add feeling or just colour? Keep or kill the look in one sentence.

## Rung 4 — Story across a full film
**Lesson:** A highlight film is a small movie: a hook in the first 10 seconds, rising tension, a release, and a callback to the opening at the end. Structure first, pretty shots second.
**Challenge:** From real or staged footage, structure a 2–3 minute highlight: hook → build → peak → resolve → callback. Storyboard the five beats before you cut.
**Deliverable:** One 2–3 minute highlight edit with a clear arc and a callback ending.
**Review:** Show it to one person who wasn't there. Did they feel the arc? Note where attention dropped.

## Rung 5 — Signature & speed
**Lesson:** Professionals are recognisable and fast. A signature (your pacing, your look, your transitions) makes you memorable; templates and presets make you profitable. Speed is what turns skill into income.
**Challenge:** Edit one more highlight using your saved looks and a project template. Time yourself. Define, in one paragraph, what makes an edit recognisably *yours*.
**Deliverable:** A portfolio-grade highlight + a one-page documented edit workflow (promote it to `library/` once it has worked twice).
**Review:** Compare this edit's time and quality to Rung 4's. Is it faster and better? You're ready to take paid work or a second-shooter slot.

## Rule
Never skip the export. A rung is complete only when something is finished and shown to one real person. Finished work is the only proof the skill moved.
