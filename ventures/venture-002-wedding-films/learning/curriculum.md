# Cinematic Editing — Venture 002 (Wedding Films)

A skill ladder you *climb by doing*. Each rung is one loop: learn a little → take real actions → hit a milestone you can show → review. You advance only when the milestone is finished and shown to one real person. By the end of this ladder you should be cutting work that looks dramatically better than where you started — not a little better, dramatically.

The runtime reads each rung's **Goal / Lesson / Do / Milestone / Review**. Everything else (Why this matters, What to observe, Success criteria, Common mistakes, Stretch challenge) is mentor commentary for you to read before you sit down to cut, not after.

---

## Rung 1 — Footage flow (organize so you can create)
*Sprint Day 3, session 3 · ~20 min · runs straight into Rung 2*

**Goal:** Turn a pile of clips into a clean timeline you can actually edit from.
**Lesson:** Editors lose hours to chaos, not to lack of talent. Before any art, build a system: import, label, and select your best shots. A small set of great clips beats a hard drive of mediocre ones — the discipline of saying no to 90% of your footage *is* the first edit you make.
**Why this matters:** Every professional editor's actual secret isn't a trick in the timeline — it's a brutal selects pass before the timeline ever opens. You're about to use today's Cinematic Shooting footage (Rung 5 coverage); the quality of your Cinematic Sequence tonight is capped by how honestly you cull right now.
**What to observe:** As you scrub your own footage, notice your instinct to keep a clip "because it took effort to shoot." Resist it. The footage doesn't know how hard it was to get.
**Do:**
- Create a new DaVinci project named with the date and "practice".
- Import today's shoot (or, if no fresh footage, free wedding b-roll from Pexels/Mixkit).
- Watch every clip once and flag your 10 best moments only.
- Drop those 10 onto the timeline in story order: arrival → details → people → emotion.
**Milestone:** A 60–90s rough "selects" timeline of your 10 best shots, exported.
**Success criteria:** Watch the rough export once, uninterrupted. If you can already sense a beginning, a middle, and an end without any trimming yet, your selects pass worked.
**Common mistakes:** Keeping a technically-flawed clip because the moment was good — fix that next time you shoot, don't fix it by lowering your bar now; selecting 20 clips "to be safe," which just moves the hard decision later instead of making it.
**Stretch challenge:** Cut your 10 down to 8 without losing the story. Notice what you didn't actually need.
**Review:** Watch it back. Do the 10 shots already tell a tiny story? Note the one weak shot to cut next time.

## Rung 2 — The cut (rhythm & story)
*Sprint Day 3, session 4 · ~40 min · study: The Quirky · ships Deliverable #2 — Cinematic Sequence*

**Goal:** Control how an edit *feels* through cut length, order, and transitions — and ship today's Cinematic Sequence.
**Lesson:** Every cinematic edit moves through beats — establish, detail, emotion, payoff. Long holds breathe; fast cuts lift energy. Cut on emotion, not on action: the instinct to cut the moment a movement finishes is a beginner's habit, professionals often cut a half-beat early or late on purpose, because the cut itself can carry feeling. Use a hard cut for momentum, a quick dip-to-black or whip for a scene change — but a transition should be earned, not decorative.
**Why this matters:** The Quirky's pacing is the reference point today specifically because their cuts are rarely on the "obvious" frame — they cut early, before an action completes, which is what makes their rhythm feel alive instead of mechanical.
**What to observe:** Count every cut in the first 30 seconds of your chosen Quirky reference. Write the exact shot length of each one down, second by second, and mark where the music's energy shifts. You are building a rhythm map, not an impression.
**Do:**
- Pick one reference film from `references.md` and watch its first 30s three times.
- Count every cut and mark where the music turns — write the shot lengths down.
- Rebuild that timing with today's shoot footage (match the rhythm, not the footage itself).
- Trim every shot by 10% and feel how the pace tightens.
**Milestone:** *(Deliverable #2 — Cinematic Sequence)* One 30–45s sequence, built from today's coverage, whose pacing matches the reference, exported.
**Success criteria:** Play yours and the reference back to back, same volume, same screen size. If a stranger couldn't immediately tell which one is the "practice" cut from rhythm alone, you've matched it. (It's fine if they can tell from footage quality — rhythm is the only thing being tested.)
**Common mistakes:** Matching shot *count* but not shot *length* — five shots that are each twice as long as the reference is not the same rhythm; cutting everything to a metronome instead of varying it, which is the single fastest way to make a cut feel robotic instead of musical.
**Stretch challenge:** Add one transition you haven't used before — a match cut on shape or motion, or a whip pan join between two of your moving shots from Shooting Rung 3.
**Review:** Watch once with eyes closed — does the sound alone carry the arc? Note one cut that fights the music.

## Rung 3 — Music, sync & sound design
*Sprint Day 5, session 1 of 2 · ~45 min · study: Iris Wedding Films*

**Goal:** Make music and sound drive emotion instead of just playing underneath the picture.
**Lesson:** Music is the spine of a wedding edit. Cut major moments onto beats; use J-cuts (sound leads the picture in) and L-cuts (sound lingers after the picture cuts away) so audio and video don't change at the exact same frame — that's what makes a cut feel woven instead of glued. A held breath of silence before a swell is one of the most powerful tools available and the most underused by beginners. Layer in source sound — laughter, footsteps, ambient room tone — under the music at low volume; pure music with zero sync sound is what separates an amateur cut from a finished one. This is also where speed ramps belong: a ramp into or out of slow motion should land exactly on a musical accent, never in the middle of a phrase.
**Why this matters:** Iris Wedding Films is assigned today specifically for sync discipline — their cuts don't just land near the beat, they land on the beat's exact attack, and their J/L-cuts are why the edit never feels like it's "waiting" for the next shot.
**What to observe:** In an Iris piece, find one J-cut and one L-cut and time exactly how many frames the audio leads or lags the picture. It's usually shorter than you'd guess — a few frames, not a few seconds.
**Do:**
- Choose one track and mark its beat grid and its single biggest swell.
- Place your strongest visual moment exactly on that swell.
- Land every hard cut on a beat for one 45–60s stretch.
- Add one J-cut and one L-cut so audio crosses the edit, and layer one source-sound moment (laughter, a door, footsteps) under the music.
**Milestone:** A 45–60s beat-synced cut with audible source sound layered in, where every major cut feels "on time."
**Success criteria:** Mute the music and play the clip with source sound only. If the scene still feels alive — not just silent and dead without the song — your sound layering passed.
**Common mistakes:** Syncing literally every cut to a beat, which becomes monotonous fast — vary it, and save the hardest on-beat hits for the moments that deserve emphasis; placing a speed ramp's peak in the middle of a held musical note instead of on its attack; forgetting source sound entirely and leaving music as the only audio in the timeline.
**Stretch challenge:** Build one speed ramp (a clip that shifts from normal to slow motion, or the reverse) timed precisely to a single musical accent.
**Review:** Watch once with eyes closed — does the sound alone carry the arc? Note one cut that fights the music.

## Rung 4 — Colour: balance, skin, and the cinematic look
*Sprint Day 5, session 2 of 2 · ~45 min · study: Daniel Schaffer · ships Deliverable #4 (with Rung 3) — Wedding-Style Edit #1*

**Goal:** Give footage a deliberate, consistent, flattering mood — and be able to reuse it instantly on the next project.
**Lesson:** Grade in three passes, in order: balance (neutral exposure and white balance on every shot) → contrast (build depth with curves) → look (commit to a mood). Skin tones are the one thing a viewer judges instantly and unconsciously — use a qualifier/mask to isolate skin if your overall grade is pushing it too warm, too orange, or too magenta; protecting skin while stylizing everything else around it is exactly what separates a "filtered" look from a "graded" one. Halation (a soft glow that blooms off bright highlights) and fine film grain are the two textures that read as "film" to a viewer's eye faster than any color choice — applied with restraint, not as a blanket Instagram filter. One consistent look beats ten clever ones, and a saved look (a DaVinci PowerGrade) makes you fast enough to do this for money.
**Why this matters:** Daniel Schaffer's grading is the reference today because it's identifiable from a single freeze-frame — warm highlights, protected skin, a touch of halation on backlit edges, visible but restrained grain. None of that happens by accident in his work, and none of it should in yours either.
**What to observe:** Freeze-frame three shots from a Daniel Schaffer piece and look specifically at skin versus background — the background often carries more color push than the skin does. That gap is the masking work.
**Do:**
- On the colour page, balance exposure and white on every shot of your Rung 3 piece.
- Add contrast with the curves until it has depth.
- Qualify or mask the skin tone in at least one shot and protect it from your overall colour push.
- Apply one deliberate look (warm filmic or soft teal-orange) plus a touch of halation and fine grain.
- Save the full grade as a reusable power-grade / LUT.
**Milestone:** *(Deliverable #4, paired with Rung 3 — Wedding-Style Edit #1)* Your Rung 3 piece, fully graded with protected skin tones, exported, plus one saved look you can reapply.
**Success criteria:** Toggle the grade on/off on the final export. The graded version should look like a deliberate creative choice with skin still looking human — not a color cast slapped over everything including faces.
**Common mistakes:** Applying a LUT directly on top of unbalanced footage (balance first, always — a look applied to bad exposure just looks like bad exposure with a tint); pushing grain or halation so hard it reads as a filter instead of a texture; never saving the grade, which means redoing this work from zero on every future edit.
**Stretch challenge:** Build a second, contrasting look (e.g., a cooler, moodier alternative) and save both — start the habit of having style options, not just one default.
**Review:** Toggle the grade on/off. Does it add feeling or just colour? Keep or kill it in one sentence.

## Rung 5 — Story across a full film
*Sprint Day 7, AM · ~75 min · study: Ramit Batra, Bollywood high-end wedding cinematography*

**Goal:** Hold attention across a 2–3 minute highlight with a real arc — Deliverable #6, Wedding-Style Edit #2.
**Lesson:** A highlight is a small movie: a hook in the first 10 seconds, rising tension, a release, and a callback to the opening at the end. Structure first, pretty shots second — a perfectly graded film with no shape will still feel long at ninety seconds, while a rough cut with real structure can hold attention for three minutes. Bollywood high-end wedding work is built on scale and choreographed reveal — a shot deliberately withheld until its moment, a wide that's earned by everything that came before it. You don't need their budget to borrow their patience.
**Why this matters:** Ramit Batra's films almost always open on something small and specific, not the wide establishing shot you'd expect — a detail, a hand, a single look — and only widen out once the emotional hook is set. That's a structural choice, not a budget one, and it's exactly reproducible on an iPhone edit.
**What to observe:** Map a Ramit Batra or comparable high-end Indian wedding film's full structure on paper: where does it open, where's the first real wide shot, where's the emotional peak, how does it end. Notice how long the film waits before showing you anything resembling a "hero shot."
**Do:**
- Write the five beats on paper: hook → build → peak → resolve → callback.
- Assemble each beat as its own mini-section before fine-cutting.
- Deliberately withhold your single best/widest shot until the peak — don't spend it early.
- Echo your opening shot at the very end (the callback).
**Milestone:** *(Deliverable #6 — Wedding-Style Edit #2)* One 2–3 minute highlight edit with a clear arc, a withheld hero shot, and a callback ending, exported.
**Success criteria:** Show it to one person who wasn't there, with no context. Afterward ask them to describe the shape of what they just watched — if they can name a beginning, a turn, and an ending without your help, the arc landed.
**Common mistakes:** Front-loading your best shot in the first 20 seconds because you're excited about it — saving it is harder and almost always better; treating "structure" as a luxury to add later instead of the first thing you plan, before a single clip is trimmed.
**Stretch challenge:** Add a second, smaller callback mid-film — a visual rhyme between the build and the resolve sections, not just the open/close bookend.
**Review:** Show it to one person who wasn't there. Did they feel the arc? Mark where attention dropped.

## Rung 6 — Signature, speed & client delivery (paid-ready)
*Sprint Day 7, PM · ~60 min · study: Hollywood montage craft, luxury commercial filmmaking · ships Deliverable #7 — Polished Portfolio Piece*

**Goal:** Edit fast, in a style that's recognisably yours, and package the result the way a paying client would actually receive it.
**Lesson:** Professionals are recognisable and fast. A signature look and habit makes you memorable; templates, saved grades, and bins make you profitable, because speed is what turns a skill into income instead of a hobby. Luxury commercial filmmaking is the reference here not for its budget but for its finish discipline: consistent aspect ratio, clean titles if any, no stray frames at heads/tails, correct loudness so it doesn't clip on a phone speaker, and a file named and exported the way a client expects to receive it — not "final_final_v3.mov."
**Why this matters:** The gap between "good edit" and "edit someone pays for" is almost never raw skill at this stage — it's whether the file that lands in someone's inbox looks and behaves like something a professional sent.
**What to observe:** Watch one luxury commercial spot and notice everything that *isn't* the footage: how it opens (no slow fade-up wasting the first second), how consistent the grade is shot-to-shot, how it ends (a clean last frame, not an awkward trail-off).
**Do:**
- Build a project template with your saved looks, titles, and bins.
- Regrade your Rung 5 highlight using only the template and your saved look from Rung 4, and time yourself doing it.
- Export at the correct spec for where it's going (vertical for Instagram, correct loudness, no stray head/tail frames) and name the file as you would for a client.
- Write one paragraph defining what makes an edit recognisably *yours*.
- Post the finished piece publicly (Instagram/Vimeo) as portfolio.
**Milestone:** *(Deliverable #7 — Polished Portfolio Piece)* A portfolio-grade highlight posted publicly + a one-page documented workflow (promote to `library/` once it's worked twice).
**Success criteria:** Compare this edit's time-to-finish and quality against Rung 5's raw cut. If it's both faster and better, the template and signature are real, not aspirational.
**Common mistakes:** Treating "signature style" as a fixed filter instead of a repeatable set of decisions (shot rhythm, grade, music taste) — a signature should survive being applied to completely different footage; skipping the export-spec check and shipping a file that's the wrong aspect ratio or too quiet on a phone speaker, which undoes a week of craft in the first three seconds.
**Stretch challenge:** Cut a 15-second vertical teaser from the same footage, edited for Instagram's attention span specifically rather than just trimmed down from the long version — notice how different the rhythm needs to be.
**Review:** Compare this edit's time and quality to Rung 5's. Faster and better? You're ready for a paid edit or a second-shooter slot.
