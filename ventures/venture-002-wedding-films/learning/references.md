# Reference Films — Venture 002 (Wedding Films)

Films to study by **reconstruction and analysis**, not admiration. The goal is never to copy a shot — it's to understand *how these editors and shooters think*: what they noticed on the day, why they cut where they cut, why they waited before showing a shot, why a moment of emotion lands the way it does. Watching passively teaches you nothing. Breaking a sequence down to its frame counts and rebuilding its timing in DaVinci teaches you everything.

This week's curriculum (`curriculum.md`, `shooting.md`) assigns specific filmmakers to specific rungs, on purpose — each one is the clearest teacher of exactly one skill. Don't binge all of them; study the assigned one deeply on the assigned day.

## How to study a reference
1. Watch once, full screen, no notes — just feel where it moves you, and notice the moment it does.
2. Watch again counting cuts: how long is each shot? Where does the music turn? Write the shot lengths down, second by second.
3. Note the **opening 3 shots** and the **first emotional beat** — what was withheld, and for how long?
4. Rebuild that sequence's *timing* with your own or stock footage. Match the rhythm, not the footage.
5. Ask, in writing, one question this rung's lesson is built around (see the table below) — answer it from evidence in the film, not impression.

## Where to find these
- **YouTube / Vimeo:** search each name directly, plus "wedding film", "wedding teaser." For Bollywood high-end work, search top Indian wedding filmmakers' teaser reels — study the editing and lighting decisions, not the budget you don't have.
- **Instagram Reels:** "#weddingfilm", "#cinematicwedding" — short-form pacing is closest to what you'll sell first, and closest to Iris's and The Quirky's native format.
- **Luxury commercial / Hollywood:** title sequences and montage scenes for Rung 6 — the finish-quality bar, not a shot-list to copy.
